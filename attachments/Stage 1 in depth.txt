## Stage 1 — Complete Conceptual Summary

### Why Welford's Algorithm

Naive running variance accumulates a sum and sum-of-squares, then subtracts them — causing catastrophic cancellation for large traffic volumes, potentially producing negative variance. Welford updates mean and variance incrementally using only small deltas, keeping numbers stable at all times. Constant memory (`n`, `mean`, `M2`), constant time per update, no external crate needed.

**Golden test case:** `{4, 7, 13, 16}` → `mean = 10.0`, `variance = 30.0`. Your Rust implementation must reproduce this exactly.

### Why EWMA

Simple Moving Average weights all samples equally — sluggish during a flood ramp-up. EWMA weights recent samples exponentially more, reacting faster to sudden spikes. Stores exactly one value regardless of history length. No crate needed.

### Why Shannon Entropy

Raw packet count can't distinguish DDoS from a flash crowd. Unique IP count misses distribution shape — two windows with identical unique IP counts can have completely different concentration patterns. Shannon entropy (`-Σ p(x)log₂p(x)`) captures the full probability distribution of source IPs in one scalar. Computed using `HashMap` for frequency counting and `f64::log2()` — standard library only.

### Why They're Complementary

| Scenario | EWMA Rate | Entropy |
|---|---|---|
| Normal traffic | Low, stable | High |
| Volumetric flood (many sources) | Spikes | Drops slightly |
| Single-source flood | Spikes hard | Drops hard |
| Flash crowd (legitimate surge) | Spikes | Stays high |
| Low-and-slow attack | Stays low | May drop |

EWMA catches volume anomalies. Entropy catches distribution anomalies. Welford makes both thresholds adaptive to your specific network's baseline — that adaptivity is the core novelty of the system.

---

### The Three Layers After Capture

**Layer 1 — Per packet:**
- EWMA updates its rate estimate
- Source IP incremented in `HashMap`

**Layer 2 — Per window (every 50th packet):**
- EWMA read as snapshot → scalar `r`
- Entropy computed from `HashMap` → scalar `h`
- `HashMap` clears, packet counter resets
- Note: entropy resets every window. EWMA never resets — it carries memory across windows by design.

**Layer 3 — Per window, immediately after Layer 2:**
- `Welford(r).update(r)` → checks `r > mean_r + 2σ_r`
- `Welford(h).update(h)` → checks `h < mean_h - 2σ_h` (entropy drops during attack)
- Either triggering → signal Stage 2

Welford's `n` counts **windows**, not packets. After 50 packets, Welford has seen exactly one sample.

Every 50th packet closes the window: entropy is computed from the IP counter, EWMA is read as a snapshot, both scalars are fed into their respective Welford accumulators, both are compared against their dynamic `μ + 2σ` boundary, and if either breaches it, Stage 2 is signalled.

This specific set of formulas is **Welford’s Algorithm** for computing a running variance. It is a brilliant way to track how "spread out" your data is without having to remember every single number you've seen.

To explain this line by line, let’s use a simple analogy: **You are tracking the heights of people walking into a room.** ---

### Line 1: `n += 1`

* **Analogy:** A clicker counter at the door.
* **Explanation:** A new person ($x$) walks into the room. You press the clicker counter. `n` is simply the total number of people standing in the room right now.

### Line 2: `delta = x - mean`

* **Analogy:** The "Surprise Factor."
* **Explanation:** Before this new person walked in, you had an idea of the average height of everyone already in the room (`mean`). Now you look at the new person ($x$). `delta` is how much taller or shorter they are compared to what you expected. If the room average is 5'8" and a 6'0" person walks in, your "surprise factor" (`delta`) is $+4$ inches.
* *Note: This measures distance from the **old** average before we change it.*

### Line 3: `mean += delta / n`

* **Analogy:** Nudging the baseline.
* **Explanation:** You need to update your average height, but you can't let the new person completely derail it. If there are 10 people in the room (`n = 10`), that 4-inch surprise (`delta`) gets divided equally among all 10 people. You nudge the room average up by just $4 / 10 = 0.4$ inches.

### Line 4: `delta2 = x - mean`

* **Analogy:** The "New Reality Check."
* **Explanation:** Now that the average has moved up a bit to accommodate the tall person, you look at them again. They are still tall, but because the average moved closer to them, they don't look *quite* as dramatically tall as they did a second ago. If the new average is now 5'8.4", their distance from this *new* average (`delta2`) is $+3.6$ inches.

### Line 5: `M2 += delta * delta2`

* **Analogy:** The "Spread Aggregator" (The Piggy Bank).
* **Explanation:** `M2` is like a piggy bank where you accumulate a score of how chaotic or spread out the heights are.
* If you multiply a big old surprise (`delta`) by a big new surprise (`delta2`), you get a **massive number** to throw into the piggy bank. This means the room is becoming much more diverse in height.
* If a person walks in who is exactly average, both deltas are 0. $0 \times 0 = 0$, so you add nothing to the piggy bank. The room's variance stays calm.
* *Why multiply them?* Multiplying a negative by a negative (if someone is super short) results in a **positive** number. This ensures that being exceptionally short and exceptionally tall *both* increase the spread score.



### Line 6: `variance = M2 / (n - 1)`

* **Analogy:** Sharing the chaos (with a tax).
* **Explanation:** To get the final variance (the official measurement of how "spread out" the room is), you take the total chaos stored in the `M2` piggy bank and divide it by the number of people.
* **Why `n - 1` instead of `n`?** This is called **Bessel's Correction**. Think of it as a "small sample tax." If you've only seen a few people, you don't truly know the variance of the whole world yet. Dividing by $n-1$ instead of $n$ makes the final variance slightly *larger*, which acts as a mathematical safety margin to account for the fact that you are guessing the variance based on a limited group rather than the entire population.


You hit the nail on the head. That interaction between $\delta$ (pre-shift error) and $\delta_2$ (post-shift error) is the entire secret sauce of Welford’s algorithm.

Multiplying them together ($\delta \times \delta_2$) perfectly balances the books algebraically. If you only used the old mean and squared it ($\delta^2$), you would overshoot because you haven't accounted for how much the mean moved. If you used the new mean and squared it ($\delta_2^2$), you would undershoot. The product of the two is the exact geometric correction needed to transition the sum of squares from the old mean to the new mean without storing past data or losing precision to catastrophic cancellation.

And your gut check is spot on: if a data point lands perfectly on the current average, it introduces zero surprise, shifts nothing, and injects exactly zero chaos into $M_2$.

#### Layer 1: The Windowed Estimators (Time/Count Bound)

This layer deals with the high-frequency chaos of network traffic. It acts as a lens, focusing raw packet data into a single descriptive micro-metric over a fixed window (e.g., 50 packets).

* **Pipeline A (Entropy):** Collects source IPs over 50 packets, calculates the probability distribution, and outputs a single scalar value representing the diversity of your traffic.
* **Pipeline B (EWMA Rate):** Tracks packet arrival intervals, applies the exponential smoothing factor to get a localized throughput metric, and outputs a single scalar value representing current velocity.

#### Layer 2: The Welford Accumulators (The Baseline Holders)

Once every 50 packets, Layer 1 hands off exactly two numbers to Layer 2: `entropy_score` and `ewma_rate_score`.

* You have two completely independent Welford state objects running in memory.
* They don't care about packets, IPs, or time. They only care that they just received their $n$-th scalar sample. They run the dual-delta update, increment $n$, and update their internal running $\mu$ and $M_2$.

#### Layer 3: The Boundary Evaluator (The Gatekeeper)

Using the updated states from Layer 2, you compute the current standard deviation ($\sigma = \sqrt{M_2 / (n-1)}$).

* You compare the *current* window's scalar score against the newly calculated threshold: $\mu + 2\sigma$.
* If the current entropy drops significantly below $\mu - 2\sigma$ (indicating a highly concentrated DDoS attack from a spoofed/single source) or the EWMA rate spikes past $\mu + 2\sigma$, the gateway flags an anomaly.

### Key Implementation Insight for your Gateway

Because Welford relies on an accurate $n$ to calculate the variance correctly, you have to decide if your baseline is **infinite** or **rolling**.

If your gateway runs for weeks, $n$ will become massive. While Welford is numerically stable enough to handle this, a network baseline changes between 3:00 AM and 3:00 PM. If $n$ is 1,000,000, a massive DDoS attack at noon won't nudge the mean or variance enough to trigger Layer 3 quickly, because the "nudge factor" ($\frac{\delta}{n}$) will be practically zero.

To fix this, most production gateways use a **Modified Welford** or a sliding window variance that caps $n$ (or mimics an EWMA for the variance itself) so the algorithm maintains "recency memory" and doesn't become rigid over time.

* **`delta2`** measures how far $x$ still is from the center *after the center stepped toward it*.

* **Low Score (Anomalous):** If you check 50 IDs and all 50 belong to the exact same person, that is highly concentrated and suspicious. The Shannon Entropy formula outputs a score close to **0**.
* **High Score (Normal/Healthy):** If you check 50 IDs and they belong to 45 different people, the traffic is highly diverse and well-distributed. The formula outputs a high score (closer to **5**).
